DJ Booking Spreadsheet: Why It's Costing You Money (And What to Use Instead)

By GigOS Team · 13 Jul 2026 · 6 min read

Every DJ starts with a spreadsheet. A Google Sheet with columns for venue, date, fee, advance paid, advance balance. It works — until it doesn't.

The problem isn't the spreadsheet itself. It's that a spreadsheet is a passive tool. It stores data you put in, but it doesn't think for you. It won't remind you that an advance is overdue. It won't tell you a venue is about to forget you exist. It won't write your invoice in ten seconds.

If you're managing more than five or six active bookings at a time and still running everything through a gig booking spreadsheet, you're doing more admin than you need to — and probably losing money in the gaps.

Here's exactly what it's costing you.

The 5 Hidden Costs of a DJ Booking Spreadsheet

  • 01

    Missed advance payments

    Promoters delay advances because most DJs don't follow up until they're at the venue door. Your spreadsheet won't alert you when an advance is overdue. You have to remember to check it, notice it's late, find the promoter's number, and write a chase message from scratch. Most of the time, you don't. That money sits in their account for weeks — sometimes months.

  • 02

    Lost rebookings

    A venue that books you once will book you again — if you stay visible. But a spreadsheet has no concept of time passing. If you played Club X six weeks ago and haven't followed up, your spreadsheet doesn't care. You forget. They move on to the next DJ who reached out. A rebook that should have been automatic slips away completely.

  • 03

    Slow, manual invoicing

    Every time you invoice a client, you either open a Word template, fill in the details, export to PDF, and send it — or you duplicate an old invoice and risk sending wrong totals. It takes 15 to 20 minutes per invoice and it's entirely manual. Multiply that across your bookings each month and you're spending hours on admin that shouldn't take that long.

  • 04

    No real income picture

    A spreadsheet can sum a column of fees. What it can't do is show you net income after expenses, tell you which gig types pay best, compare this month to last, or highlight which venues are worth prioritising. You have raw numbers but no business insight — so you can't make smart decisions about where to focus your energy.

  • 05

    No pipeline view

    At any moment you have gigs at different stages: one that's just an enquiry, two that are confirmed but advances haven't come in, one that's done but not yet invoiced, and three that are fully paid. A spreadsheet shows all of these as flat rows. You need to scan the whole thing to understand where your attention needs to go — which means things fall through the cracks.

Quick estimate: If you're playing 10 gigs a month and missing even one advance per month due to poor tracking, and each advance averages even a modest amount — that's money you earned but had to chase late, interest-free, on someone else's terms. A proper gig management system pays for itself immediately.
